Foundational Languages

The State of Indigenous Languages

The words we use to describe our mother tongues carry a history of power. Across Africa, the division between an "official" language and a "vernacular" language is not an accident—it is the deliberate structural legacy of colonial administration. European languages were established as the exclusive currency for law, wealth, and governance, while indigenous mother tongues were intentionally pushed to the margins. Today, this institutional neglect is actively maintained by modern school systems, policymakers, and a society that continues to exclude foundational languages from formal learning and professional spaces. Because of this, newer generations are forced to rely almost entirely on foreign languages for academic validation and social success. This creates a devastating point of friction: When a native tongue dies out, a community does not just lose words; it loses the primary vessel keeping its entire culture alive. The loss of these languages strips away ancestral folklore, traditional knowledge systems, and deeply nuanced oral histories. Without their foundational languages, newer generations are left culturally dispossessed—alienated from the specific proverbs, untranslatable humor, and spiritual philosophies that form the very bedrock of their identity. This website is an advocacy canvas to confront this loss, challenge these structures, and fight for linguistic preservation.
